The Book of Amos

The History of the Book of Amos

The Book of Amos, part of the twelve minor prophets in the Old Testament, was written around 760 - 750 BCE during a time of prosperity in the northern kingdom of Israel, under the reign of Jeroboam II. This was a period of material wealth, but the people had become spiritually complacent and morally corrupt. Amos was a shepherd and dresser of sycamore trees, in the southern kingdom of Judah, yet his calling was to deliver a message of warning and repentance to the northern kingdom.

Amos’s background as a humble laborer illustrates an important principle: God calls individuals to fulfill His purposes, regardless of their social or economic standing. His message came during a time when the people of Israel believed their wealth and status were evidence of God’s favor. However, Amos warned that their outward prosperity masked deep spiritual decay, and they were neglecting their covenant relationship with God. His prophecies preceded the eventual destruction of Israel by the Assyrians in 722 BCE, showing that no nation is immune to the consequences of forsaking God’s commandments.

Key Characters

  • Amos: The central figure of the book, Amos was not from a prophetic lineage but was called by God as a shepherd and farmer. His humble origins highlight a key truth, God often chooses the weak and simple to confound the wise. Amos serves as an example of one who, though untrained in religious or political power, was able to fulfill a divine mission.
  • Jeroboam II: The king of Israel during Amos' time, Jeroboam presided over a period of territorial expansion and economic prosperity. His reign brought political stability and wealth to the kingdom, but spiritually, the people had drifted far from their covenants with God. Despite the outward success, the spiritual and moral fabric of the kingdom was in disarray.
  • The People of Israel: Amos addresses the whole nation of Israel but focuses on the wealthy and powerful who were oppressing the poor and engaging in unrighteous behaviors while believing their covenant status would protect them. In other words, Amos would be addressing those who believe that outward signs of prosperity or righteousness can compensate for disobedience to God’s laws.

Divine Messages, Themes, and Patterns

Justice and Righteousness: Amos emphasizes that true righteousness goes beyond outward forms of worship. In Amos 5:24, he pleads, “But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.” This reflects the need for the children of Israel to live the principles of the gospel fully, not just outwardly, but in their hearts. We can interpret this to mean that justice and righteousness must be lived through daily discipleship, caring for others, and upholding the principles of charity.

Hypocrisy: Amos rebukes the Israelites for relying on empty performances while neglecting the weightier matters of their covenants with God. Amos 5:21-23 shows that God is not pleased with their outward sacrifices and feasts because they were not accompanied by genuine repentance and righteous living. Today, this can be likened to attending Church or partaking of the sacrament without having a true change of heart and a desire to live according to covenants made at baptism.

Judgment and Accountability:Amos’s warnings of destruction serve as a reminder that covenant people are held to a higher standard. The northern kingdom believed they were immune to divine judgment because of their status as God’s chosen people. However, Amos prophesies that their failure to live up to their covenants would bring inevitable consequences. This reflects on the understanding that breaking covenants brings spiritual or even temporal consequences.

Hope and Restoration: Despite the stern warnings, Amos ends his book with a message of hope. In Amos 9:11-15, God promises to restore Israel, symbolizing the gathering of Israel in the latter days. This is also a message of hope that all people will have another chance. Our God is a God of second chances.

What the Restoration Adds to Our Understanding

A Modern Prophet’s Perspective:Amos’s role as a prophet is a strong parallel to the calling of prophets today. Just as Amos was sent to call Israel to repentance and back to their covenants, modern prophets call to renew our covenants and prepare for the Second Coming of Jesus Christ. The Restoration teaches that God has always communicated His will through prophets and that, like Amos, these prophets are called to speak the hard truths necessary for spiritual progress.

Joseph Smith Translation (JST): While there are no extensive changes in the JST for the Book of Amos, the Restoration adds clarity to the role of prophets and the consequences of breaking covenants. The prophetic tradition Amos exemplifies continues today, with leaders urging members to prioritize covenant relationships over worldly concerns.

The Gathering of Israel: Amos speaks of a future time when Israel will be gathered and restored. We understand this in both a physical and spiritual sense as the gathering of Israel progresses today. Through missionary work and temple ordinances, we can participate in the fulfillment of this promise.

Covenants and Accountability:The Restoration teaches us that covenants are not just promises between us and God but sacred agreements that must be upheld with integrity diligence. Amos’s calls for justice are a reminder that we must live in accordance with divine principles, including caring for the poor and obeying the laws of the land. Amos’s warning applies today in the context of living temple covenants and caring for those in need through both temporal and spiritual means.

Lessons For Today

Covenants Require Righteous Living: Amos’s message is clear that God requires more than outward acts; He requires true conversion and discipleship. Today we are reminded that keeping covenants means striving to live Christlike lives, serving others, and maintaining personal integrity.

Prophets Warn and Prepare Us: Just as Amos called the Israelites to repentance, modern prophets continue to warn and prepare the Saints for future challenges. President Russell M. Nelson, for example, has emphasized the importance of spiritual preparedness and staying close to the Lord through attending the temple.

The Gathering of Israel: The final restoration mentioned in Amos can be seen as a direct type of the gathering of Israel in these latter days. We are all called to participate in this gathering through missionary work and the sealing power of the temple. Amos’s prophecies offer hope that no matter how far individuals or nations stray, God’s promises of restoration and redemption remain sure for those who turn back to Him.

Caring for the Poor and Needy: Amos’s powerful indictment of social injustice is a call to action. The Law of tithing and the Church’s welfare program reflect the eternal principle of caring for the poor and ensuring that wealth and resources are shared with those in need.
